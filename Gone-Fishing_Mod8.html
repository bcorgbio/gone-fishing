Effects of Temperature and Wind on Arrival Time of Trans-Gulf Migrants

Gone-Fishing

2022-12-15

Introduction

The term trans-Gulf migrants or TGMs, refer to species of birds that fly non-stop over the Gulf of Mexico and arrive on land between Texas and Florida. They are part of a larger group of birds that undertake long-distance migrations from Central and South America to more temperate latitudes.

The time of arrival of these TGMs have quite a large effect on their rates of survival. One example of this is the red knot, or Calidris canutus rufa. In the spring, this species of bird travels from Tierra del Fuego to the Delaware Bay (a 30,000 km trip), and usually bulks up on horseshoe crab eggs in Delaware Bay before leaving to breed in Arctic polar deserts. From 1997-2002, a higher proportion of knots did not reach threshold departure masses, and the population size dropped from 51,000 to 37,000. This drop in population is assumed to be a result of birds arriving later in Delaware, after the horseshoe crab eggs had already been depleted. (Baker et al. (2004))

Other research has also shown that survival rates are reduced by cold weather and high density, (Cook et al. (2021)) and as climate change continues to cause abnormal temperature and weather fluctuation, TGM survival rate also fluctuates.

In 2002, Cornell Laboratory of Ornithology and the National Audubon society created a program called eBird that allows citizen scientists and birders to share their observations, creating a database for bird sightings. The creation of this program has the potential to be an incredibly important tool in the study of avian biology.

Our goal with this project is to use eBird and meteorological data to understand the effect of local weather conditions when TGMs arrive in Massachusetts.

Methods

First we chose five trans-Gulf migrant (TGM) bird species to study, each bird species being from different families. The species we chose are as follows: Common Nighthawk (Chordeiles minor), Scarlet Tanager (Piranga olivacea), Cerulean Warbler (Setophaga cerulea), Baltimore Oriole (Icterus galbula), and Red-eyed vireo (Vireo olivaceus). Using the occ_data() function from rgbif, which searches GBIF occurrences, we then download the occurrence data for these species. We then used logistic modeling operations to predict the Julian day when 25% of the population arrives, aka the arrival time, for each year and for each species. Three locations were chosen along the assumed flight path of the birds, Mobile, AL being on the Gulf at the beginning of the route, Boston, MA being the end destination, and Charlotte, NC being about the middle of the journey. We used weather data from the NOAA website with the parameters of minimum and maximum temperature in tenths of degrees celsius (tmin), average wind velocity in ms (awnd), and direction of fastest 2-minute wind (wdf2). To evaluate the effect of weather parameters on arrival time we used a linear mixed-effect modeling using lme4 package and lme4() function. Lastly we use the anova() function from the car package to look at the interactions and assesses the significance of the fixed effects.
